Regex Atomic Group Demystified

“An atomic group is a group that, when the regex engine exits from it, automatically throws away all backtracking positions remembered by any tokens inside the group. Atomic groups are non-capturing.”

For a better comprehension, let’s experiment with some examples.

Match (?>\d+)2 against “42”, RegexBuddy fails after two attempts.

Watch closely how it fails at first attempt. Quantifier + is greedy by default, eating as much digits as possible, leaving no chance for 2 to match for no characters are left in the testing string. Then it reports failure immediately for the attempt at first character WITHOUT BACKTRACKING.

This is exactly how possessive quantifier works in Java, it’s equivalent to \d++2. Now you should be able to figure out why
(?>at|ato)m fails to match “atom” but ((?>at)|ato)m succeeds.

So the key point of atomic group is to suppress backtracking behavior while not keeping the matching group.

Regex Prime Test Revisited

Walking through the prime test means you have mastered the basics of Regex, which should be in every developer’s toolkit regardless of the language your are programming in.
Now let’s make a comparison between ^(s{2,})\1+$ and ^(s{2,}?)\1+$. Both could test the prime nature of a number, but varies significantly when it comes to efficiency.
The root cause is that quantifier(including . * + ? {num, num}) is greedy by default, consuming as many characters as possible. In contrast, reluctant quantifier only consumes when it fails to match.

To understand greediness of quantifier, the following is the matching details when testing against number 9(skipped ^$ matching):

step position in regex position in string(matched) result
1 p p match
2 p{2,} ppppppppp match
3 (p{2,})\1+ ppppppppp fail
4 p{2,} (backtracking) pppppppp match
5 (p{2,})\1+ pppppppp fail
6 p{2,} (backtracking) ppppppp match
7 (p{2,})\1+ ppppppp fail
8 p{2,} (backtracking) pppppp match
9 (p{2,})\1+ pppppp fail
10 p{2,} (backtracking) ppppp match
11 (p{2,})\1+ ppppp fail
12 p{2,} (backtracking) pppp match
13 (p{2,})\1+ pppppppp fail
14 p{2,} (backtracking) ppp match
15 (p{2,})\1+ ppppppppp match

Clear now? The performance difference is roughly equivalent to the following question:

Which is faster to test whether a number is prime?

Actually, if you know some maths, the known regex could be improved further to ^(p{2,t}?)\1+,where t = floor(sqrt(n)).
This greatly reduces the number of attempts in failed match cases.

Understanding Regex Prime Test

I saw some delicate test for prime numbers, using regular expression only. Here’s the idea,

Sounds tricky? Not actually. Creating the number of characters should be fine, let’s examine what the regex is all
about. Literally, the regex reads one or the number could be divided by any of 2,3,…. I will explain the latter part ^(p{2,}?)\1+$ in details.

  • ^$ indicates it’s designed to match the string as a whole
  • (p{2,}?) matches p at least 2 times, but will match reluctantly, the content will be captured as group 1
  • \1+ is a back reference to capturing group 1, the net effect is to multiple it by 2 or more times.

Now it’s time to illustrate each step when matching against 9 to help you understand:

step position in regex position in string(matched) result
1 p p match
2 p{2,}? pp match
3 (p{2,}?)\1+ pppppppp fail
4 (p{2,}?)\1+ (backtracking) pppppp fail
5 (p{2,}?)\1+ (backtracking) pppp fail
6 p{2,}? (backtracking) ppp match
7 (p{2,}?)\1+ ppppppppp match
